Mirror

Mirror is an established fashion brand that caters to the everyday clothing needs of children through 65 year olds.The design breif consisted of overhauling their brand identity and to help establish their online e-commerce presence to support their brick and mortar operations.

Interviews

Five in-person interviews were conducted. The age range of the interviews was 22 to 28 with a gender break down of three females and two males.From these interviews I understood 5 things:

  • Online option overload is a barrier to purchasing.
  • Product filters are important.
  • Browsing online and purchasing offline is common.
  • “Would the clothing fit?”
  • Loyalty depended on the ease of purchasing and discounts.

Persona

Taking into account the findings from primary research I identified one core persona. I believe there are more, such as ‘the parent shopper’, but this wasn’t evident from my interviews.

Sarah—a mid twenties marketing manager, a few years into her career. She wants to look the part, is strapped for time and has a budget she likes to stick to.

Branding & Color

Based on user research, 80% of the sample base stated that they really liked clean and minimalist interfaces.

The core colors chosen were navy blue and a complementary maroon which symbolize depth and tradition. I coupled these with shades of grey and used the core colors sparingly to enable the negative space and photography to stand out.

The typography selection of a sans-serif font (montserrat) was made to give a fresh feel to the website and break away from the traditonal feeling a serif font can give.

The message of these selections is that Mirror is an established brand that is in touch with the times. Mirror is showcasing to their existing consumer base that they’re a progressive retailer that is rooted in the fundamentals that made their brand strong.

User Testing

The hi-fidelity wireframes were linked together using InVision and tested on a new set of users.

User testing uncovered two issues—Design inconsistency’s regarding the “add to bag” page were noted by most and trouble with the design tool and the fact that only a certain user flow could be accessed.

The second was an issue of time, the first however could be rectified easily.

Reflection & Closing Comments

There are a few things I would improve with this case study:

For interviews I would like to first hand watch a users interaction with purchasing a product online.

For user testing—encouraging a higher level of commentary, and asking probing questions about each page as the test progressed. Also some kind of warm-up activity or question to put the user at ease. I noticed that most were unsure and reserved in their actions and statements.
